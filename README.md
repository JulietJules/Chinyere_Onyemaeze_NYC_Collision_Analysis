# Chinyere_Onyemaeze_NYC_Collision_Analysis
Power BI analysis of NYC motor vehicle collisions (Jan–Aug 2020), focusing on accident trends, high-risk periods, locations, and contributing factors.

NYC Motor Vehicle Collisions Analysis (Jan–Aug 2020)

Project Overview                   
This project analyzes motor vehicle collisions reported by the New York City Police Department between January and August 2020. Each record represents a single collision and includes information on date, time, location, vehicles involved, victims, and contributing factors.
The objective of this analysis is to identify accident trends, high-risk periods, locations, and contributing factors using Power BI.

Source: NYC_Collision Dataset

Analysis Objectives         
The analysis seeks to answer the following questions:
1. How are accidents distributed by month, and are there seasonal patterns?
2. When do accidents occur most frequently by day of week and hour of day?
3. Which street recorded the highest number of accidents, and what percentage of total accidents does it represent?
4. What are the most common contributing factors to accidents and fatal accidents?

Tools & Technologies: 
Power BI 
DAX (Data Analysis Expressions) 
Dashboard
Power Query 
GitHub (Documentation)

Analysis:
1. Yes, there are seasonal patterns. Accident frequency dropped significantly between March and May 2020, coinciding with COVID-19 lockdown restrictions. Accident levels increased again from June onward as mobility resumed.
2. Accidents occur most frequently on weekdays.Peak accident hours align with morning and evening commuting periods and this indicates that traffic congestion during work-related travel plays a major role in accident occurrence.
3. The street with the highest number of accidents accounted for only a small percentage of total citywide collisions. This suggests that accidents are broadly distributed across NYC rather than concentrated in one location.
4. Driver inattention or distraction was identified as the most common contributing factor for both total accidents and fatal accidents, highlighting the role of human behavior in road safety risks.

Additional Insights
1. Reduced traffic volume directly impacts accident frequency.
2. Commuting hours are consistently high-risk periods.
3. Behavioral factors contribute more to severe accidents than environmental factors.

Recommendations
1. Increase public awareness campaigns against distracted driving.
2. Improve traffic monitoring during peak commuting hours.
3. Implement targeted safety interventions on identified high-risk streets.

Conclusion  
This project demonstrates how data visualization and analysis can be used to uncover meaningful insights from traffic collision data. The findings emphasize the influence of mobility patterns and driver behavior on road safety outcomes in New York City.
